<h1>Best Places to Visit in China: A Kiwi Traveller's Guide</h1>

<p>The best places to visit in China for New Zealand and Australian travellers include Beijing, Xi'an, Shanghai, Guilin, Zhangjiajie, Chengdu, and the ancient Silk Road corridor stretching from Lanzhou to Urumqi — each offering a completely different face of the world's most layered destination. Whether you're chasing UNESCO heritage sites, dramatic karst landscapes, or markets that have been trading for two millennia, China rewards those who arrive with a good guide and genuine curiosity. As New Zealand's longest-running China specialist, <strong>CTS Tours</strong> has been introducing Kiwis to all of these destinations since 1928 — and the list below reflects nearly a century of on-the-ground knowledge.</p>

<h2>Beijing: Where Ancient and Modern China Collide</h2>

<p>No first-time visitor to China should skip Beijing, and even seasoned travellers keep finding reasons to return. The Forbidden City is unmissable — 180 acres of imperial history that photographs can never fully prepare you for. But the real magic lies in the hutongs, the ancient lane networks threaded between courtyard homes where locals still hang washing, play chess, and argue about football. A private walking tour through Dongcheng's hutongs at dusk, followed by a courtyard dinner, is the kind of evening that stays with you for years.</p>

<p>Then there's the Great Wall. Most visitors see Badaling — and it's impressive, no question. But <strong>CTS Tours</strong> can arrange access to lesser-known sections where you can stand in the early morning mist with barely another soul in sight. That's the difference between ticking off a landmark and actually experiencing it.</p>

<p>If you're weighing up your first China itinerary, the <a href="https://www.ctstours.co.nz/campaigns/october-2026/tale-of-two-cities">October 2026 Beijing & Xi'an departure</a> offers a beautifully sequenced introduction to both northern China's imperial heart and its ancient dynastic origins — a natural pairing for first-timers.</p>

<h2>Xi'an: Two Thousand Years Beneath Your Feet</h2>

<p>Xi'an was once the eastern terminus of the Silk Road — the city where caravans from Central Asia arrived laden with silk, spices, and ideas. That history is everywhere: in the Muslim Quarter's labyrinthine food alleys, in the ancient city walls you can cycle at sunset, and most famously, in the Terracotta Army — arguably the single most astonishing archaeological discovery of the 20th century.</p>

<p>Standing before those 8,000 clay soldiers, still mostly buried beneath the fields where farmers found them in 1974, produces a particular kind of silence. Xi'an is also the natural launch point for anyone venturing further west — which brings us to one of China's most undervisited and extraordinary travel routes.</p>

<h2>The Silk Road Corridor: China's Greatest Adventure</h2>

<p>If you've already done Beijing and Shanghai, or if you're the kind of traveller who wants the road less taken, the ancient Silk Road is in a category of its own. From Xi'an, the route moves west through Lanzhou and the extraordinary Bingling Temple grottoes, across the psychedelic Zhangye Danxia rainbow mountains (a landscape so surreal it looks like a geological fever dream), through Dunhuang's Mogao Caves — 1,000 years of Buddhist art carved into desert cliffs — past the Flaming Mountains of Turpan, and finally to Urumqi in Xinjiang.</p>

<h2>Shanghai: China's Most Cosmopolitan City</h2>

<p>Shanghai is a city that moves fast and looks good doing it. The Bund — that famous waterfront promenade of colonial-era banking houses facing a skyline of futuristic towers — is one of the great urban views on earth, best appreciated at dusk when both sides of the Huangpu River glow. Beyond the view, Shanghai rewards exploration: the French Concession's tree-lined streets, the traditional Yuyuan Garden tucked amid tea houses and craft stalls, and a dining scene that rivals any city in Asia.</p>

<p>For those keen to combine Shanghai with day trips to classical water towns like Suzhou or Tongli, the <a href="https://www.ctstours.co.nz/campaigns/october-2026/shanghai-surroundings">October 2026 Shanghai & Surroundings departure</a> is a well-paced option that shows you how much the region rewards a slower, more curious approach.</p>

<h2>Guilin & Yangshuo: The Landscape That Inspired a Thousand Paintings</h2>

<p>The karst mountains of Guilin are what most Kiwis picture when they imagine China's natural scenery — and they genuinely look like that. Limestone peaks rising from flat rice paddies, cormorant fishermen on bamboo rafts, mist settling into the valleys at dawn. A slow Li River cruise from Guilin to Yangshuo remains one of the great half-day experiences in Asia, unhurried and visually extraordinary from start to finish.</p>

<p>October and November are ideal months to visit — clear skies, lower humidity, and crowds that have thinned from the summer peak. The light in autumn turns the paddies golden and gives those already-dramatic peaks an added warmth that makes every photograph feel effortless.</p>

<h2>Chengdu: Giant Pandas, Spice, and Slow Living</h2>

<p>Chengdu moves to its own rhythm. This is the city where locals spend entire mornings in teahouses playing mahjong, where Sichuan cuisine reaches its highest, most lip-numbing expression, and where you can spend a morning watching giant pandas at the Chengdu Research Base in a setting that feels surprisingly intimate rather than zoo-like. Chengdu is also the gateway to Leshan — home to a 71-metre stone Buddha carved into a clifftop above a river confluence — and to the dramatic Jiuzhaigou valley, one of China's most otherworldly natural parks.</p>

<h2>Zhangjiajie: The Real Avatar Mountains</h2>

<p>The quartzite sandstone pillars of Zhangjiajie look precisely as unreal as they did in the film that borrowed them — vast columns of rock draped in subtropical forest, disappearing into cloud. The glass-bottomed Zhangjiajie Grand Canyon Bridge, the Bailong Elevator ascending a sheer cliff face, the cable cars threading between peaks — this is a park designed, seemingly, for people who want proof that the natural world can still astonish.</p>

<h2>Planning Your China Trip from New Zealand or Australia</h2>

<p>A few practical things worth knowing: New Zealand passport holders currently enjoy 15-day visa-free access to China for tourism, which suits shorter itineraries to Shanghai, Beijing, or Guilin. For longer journeys — including the Silk Road — a standard tourist visa is straightforward to obtain and opens up the full country. Language is less of a barrier than most first-timers expect, particularly in major cities, and even more so when you're travelling with a specialist operator whose guides are local, bilingual, and deeply connected to the places they show you.</p>

<p>The best times to visit China for Kiwis are generally April–May and September–November, when temperatures are comfortable, skies are clearer, and the light — particularly in autumn — is simply beautiful. Avoid peak Chinese public holidays (Golden Week in October and the Lunar New Year period) unless your itinerary is specifically designed around them.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions: Visiting China from New Zealand</h2>

<h3>Do New Zealanders need a visa to visit China?</h3>
<p>New Zealand passport holders currently enjoy 15-day visa-free entry to China for tourism purposes. For longer trips or travel to certain regions including Xinjiang, a standard tourist visa (L visa) is required. CTS Tours provides up-to-date visa guidance as part of their pre-departure support.</p>

<h3>What is the best time of year for Kiwis to visit China?</h3>
<p>April to May and September to November are the sweet spots — mild temperatures, lower rainfall, and beautiful light for photography. October is particularly spectacular in Guilin, Beijing, and along the Silk Road. Summer can be hot and humid; winter is best suited to northern China if you're chasing dramatic landscapes in the snow.</p>

<h3>How long should I spend in China on a first visit?</h3>
<p>A minimum of 12–14 days allows you to comfortably cover two or three major destinations without feeling rushed. For a more comprehensive journey — such as the Silk Road corridor from Xi'an to Urumqi — 18 days is ideal. <h3>Is China safe for independent travellers from New Zealand?</h3>
<p>China is generally very safe for international tourists. The main challenges are language and logistics — navigating transport, booking accommodation in remote areas, and finding the kinds of experiences that aren't on the tourist map. Travelling with a specialist like CTS Tours removes these barriers entirely, with local guides, pre-arranged logistics, and 98 years of operational experience behind every departure.</p>
